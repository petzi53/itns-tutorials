Learning Outcomes

We will start every chapter with Learning Outcomes (LOs). In principle, LOs are just statements about what students should be able to do by the end of a learning module (lecture, course, chapter of a book, degree course, etc.). To accommodate the appropriate level, LOs have to be stated on different levels of abstraction. To evade losing overview and drowning in too many different skills, a limitation to three to five LOs is recommendable.

The crux is to formulate LOs in a way that they can be verified. So they should use verbs for activities that can be observed. Instead of ‚Äúcan understand‚Äù or ‚Äúknows,‚Äù it is better to say ‚Äúcan explain‚Äù or ‚Äúcan give examples,‚Äù etc.

Take-home messages

As in the book, each chapter of the interactive tutorials starts with pointers to what it contains and closes with summary take-home messages. But this summary is an interactive exercise where you have to choose from different key messages. Pause, reflect, and discuss

Educational research shows that elaborating on learning material is an effective learning technique. The book encourages on regular intervals critical thinking with questions suitable for reflection and discussion.

With these tutorials, we offer Cornell Notes as a tool to process and elaborate conveyed information. It supports learning with a three-part structure of note-taking:

  1. Take notes from a lecture, book, audio, or video. But not in a mechanical descriptive way, but decide what is essential and choose your own wording.
  2. After you wrote this excerpt, look for cues and key points. Formulate problems, generate questions, and write them into another text field.
  3. Finally, in the third field, write down your learnings as a summary and try a conclusion.

Again we provide this learning tool via an H5P content type. Try it out and learn about Cornell Notes by taking ‚úèÔ∏èCornell Notes.

üß©Making the most of these tutorials

The main reason for us to write these tutorials is to support your learning. We hope that the multitude of different exercises invites you to try them. It is not necessary to take them all, and you can certainly change our proposed order. The only sequence you should follow: Read first the corresponding part in the book because the exercises build on the book‚Äôs content. Multiple representation

To internalize and grasp new terms and ideas it is necessary to vary the context of thought and perception. Cognitive science has proofed that multiple representation is especially well suited for complex scientific concepts:3

When people are learning complicated scientific concepts, interacting with multiple forms of representation such as diagrams, graphs and equations can bring unique benefits. (Ainsworth 2008)

We will foster you understanding by presenting the same content varied in different forms, as text, graphics and video. It‚Äôs not just that we expose you passively to the material but we call for your active engagement with the content by posing puzzles and challenges. (Ainsworth 1999)
